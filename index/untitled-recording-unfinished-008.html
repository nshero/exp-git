untitled/recording (008) (Unfinished)

"I want to spend my time living life instead of recording it," I wrote as a college student, several times in my journals. In some ways the archiving project is relatively young, a living tantrum suffering through its terrible twos. Naturally, however, the sort of person who might embark on such a deranged project later is likely to discover that she has been recording all along in different ways, imprinting moments by slipping small pebbles into her pockets while watching a particularly sweet sunset. Imagining how I might film a moment of riding on the highways to our family's hunting camp up north, car winding up and around forested hills and down into a cool shaded valley, with Jewel crooning out of my headphones as I held my CD player on my lap, holding it up carefully when we hit bumpy patches of gravel to avoid skipping.

Maybe this is why I have been marking my father's decline in so many ways, from furtive photographs in his hospital room to audio-recording his specialist visits. To me it became a natural evolution as time progressed-- starting first for the necessity of preserving his health, turned and expanded as we moved beyond the point of saving his life. Before his cardiologist or nephrologist said "There's nothing more we can do for you;" before they said it again and again. We could never save his life; so what was I preserving in the meantime?

As the vascular dementia set in, he could barely remember what his doctor had told him at the appointment that morning to relay to my mother over the dinner table. Mom started trying to join him but it didn't always help: they would both attend an appointment, both listen to the exact same advice from the provider, then argue over what they heard for the next six months. For a few years we would go all three, or I would join when Mom ran out of sick days.
